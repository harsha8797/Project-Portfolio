Most people find data. I go looking for what the data is hiding.

It started during my Computer Science degree — less "how do I write this code" and more "how do I know this code is actually doing what we think it is?" That instinct led me into Quality Assurance at Accenture, where I built testing frameworks that cut manual effort by 50%. Then an MSc at Leeds sharpened it further. Then Subway — yes, the sandwich place — where I reduced operational waste by 25% and grew store revenue by 15% by doing what I do best: asking uncomfortable questions of comfortable assumptions.

Six years in, I've sat with franchise managers, development leads, and frontline staff alike. I've built dashboards nobody asked for that everyone ended up needing. And I've learned that the gap between a good insight and a useless one is almost never the data — it's whether anyone actually changes their behaviour because of it.

If your data is telling you something your team isn't ready to hear — I'm probably your person.

Selected Projects

A few things I've dug into — each one a different question I couldn't leave alone.

01

Netflix Content Strategy Analysis

Netflix has 6,000+ titles and a content budget that would make most governments nervous. So what's actually working? I dug into the full catalogue to surface which genres are quietly dominating, how strategy has shifted over the years, and what the rating distribution tells us about who Netflix is really making shows for. Less "what's on Netflix" — more "why is it on Netflix."

Python EDA Pandas Matplotlib

Loan Default Risk Analysis

Banks say they use data to make lending decisions. I wanted to see if that held up. Using a real-world dataset of applicants in India, I ran a deep SQL analysis on the financial and demographic signals that separate borrowers who repay from those who don't. Turns out the story is more nuanced — and more interesting — than a credit score alone would suggest.

SQL PostgreSQL pgAdmin Risk Analysis
↗ 03

Airbnb Seattle — What Actually Drives Revenue?

If you listed a room in Seattle tomorrow, what would make you money — the neighbourhood, the price, the photos, or relentless five-star reviews? I worked through listing data, seasonal booking patterns, and customer sentiment to find out. The answer involves all of the above, but not equally — and the gaps between host assumptions and guest behaviour are where the real insights live.

Python Sentiment Analysis Seaborn Pricing Strategy
↗ 04

HR Attrition — The Data Knew Before HR Did

People don't just quit jobs. They quit situations — and the data usually knows which ones long before HR does. I analysed 1,470 employee records across 34 variables to surface patterns behind attrition: who's leaving, from which teams, at what compensation bands, and why the work-life balance scores nobody takes seriously are actually the loudest signal in the room.

SQL HR Analytics Root Cause Analysis
